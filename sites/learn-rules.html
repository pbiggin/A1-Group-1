Understanding the Rules

When playing or watching cricket, there are a few basic rules that teams should follow to ensure safe, fair and fun gameplay.

the rules of cricket, in short:

  • Teams should consist of 11 players
  • The bowler must bowl 6 deliveries to complete an over
  • Games must have two umpires
  • Batsmen can be delivered 'Outs' which takes them out of the field untill their next turn to bat.
  • The Fielding team must have one wicket keeper.
  • Scoring is calculated through runs, but can be impacted by other game elements.

Player setup

Each team is made up of 11 players*

*Some professional teams, like the Men's Australian Cricket team (Pictured above) contain more than 11 players, but only 11 play in any game at any time.

Overs

In each game, Overs are counted after 6 bowls. At the over, the bowler must be replaced with a new bowler (No bowler can throw consecutive overs) Bowlers may rotate in again after atleast 1 bowl from another bowler. Batters may also change between overs, depending on which end of the pitch they are on when the over is called. Batters can change postion after each run. Every time the bowler bowls the ball, (Even if the striker hits the ball or not), a tally is counted, which contributes to the over.

Umpiring

standing at each end of the wicket, two umpires are required to oversee each game. Umpires count the number of balls in an over, and makes decisions about appeals, and checks if the bowler has bowled a legal delivery.

During the game, batsmen can be declared out, upon which they must leave the field and be replaced by a teammate. There are a range of ways a batsman can be declared out:

Question: How many outs are required to end an inning?

Answer: 10 Outs can be accumulated before the inning is declared over. As there are only 11 players on a team, If a fielding team scores 10 outs in an inning, the inning is over as there are no more batsmen on the batting team to fill the both batter postions in the pitch. Wicket keepers

The fielding team must have one designated wicket keeper. The wicket keeper is the only player allowed to wear pads and gloves on the field, and stands behind the opporsite end to the bowler to catch the ball.

Scores

After a specified amount of overs or Innings (Dependant on the game format) Scores are compared, and the team with the largest score wins. Beyond runs, addtional elements that contribute to the score & gameplay of cricket are:

  • Extras - awarded to the batting team upon fielding errors or penalities oncured on the fielding side. Some common examples are: wides (balls bowled too far outside of the batting player's reach), no balls (Illegal deliveries).
  • Boundaries (see batting) . Boundasry scoring adds a unique factor for the game, allowing for scoring to change quickly.
  • Strike Rate - a statistic that measures the batsman's scoring speed, often used to gain insight on player's approach and skill. A high strike rate can mean the player is attempting to gain quick runs, whilst a lower strike rate may indicate the player is taking a more defenisve & accumulative approach.

Question: Why do teams / broadcasters of cricket matches calculate their Gameplay averages?

Answer: To add more interest & allow analysis of gameplay! Cricket is a widely enjoyed game, but includes alot of tatics, skill and technique to play at a high level. Statistics provide insight on how well a player plays, their improvement and their typical batting and fielding styles (Defensive or Offensive).
