Learning cricket

Cricket, the second most popular sport worldwide, is enjoyed as an source of entertainment, exercise and team bonding. believed to originate from since the Dark ages, is a game with an array of different formats which is enjoyed by a wide range of ages, in any level of compeition, causal or professional.

Cricket is a team game, played by two competing groups of 11 players each with a Bat and a ball. The objective of Cricket is to ultimately gain a bigger score by the opposing team through "runs", with each team taking turns Batting and Fielding as one "inning". Games often include one or two innings each, however it is dependant by game factors, or formats.

Cricket at a Glance

High levels of the game are typically played on an oval including a rectangular strip in the middle of the oval, the pitch, approximately 20 metres by 3 metres wide. The wickets, two sets of three long sticks in the ground, are set at each end of the pitch. They also have horizontal small sticks laying on top of the wickets known as the bails.

Batting

In an inning, teams will get the opportunity to play two positions. The 'Batting' team's objective is to hit the ball thrown by the opposing 'fielding' team, to defend the wicket. Two batsman are up at one time, and the batsman facing the delivered ball tries to hit the ball, preventing the opposing team from knocking over the wicket. Depending on how the ball is hit, the batsmen will have two options: A defensive hit will allow the batting team defend the wicket, but limits their ability to score. An offensive hit allows for the opportunity for the pair of batsmen to run to their opposite ends of the pitch, gaining a run. Another way the batting team can gain runs (points) is to hit the ball to the boundary lines. If the ball lands on the ground and then touches the boundary, four runs are gained and if the ball lands on the ground outside the boundary, then six runs are gained.

Fielding

In the other half of the Inning, the teams will swap, with one team playing the the 'Fielding' position. The aim of the fielding team - is to use their 'Bowlers' to prevent / limit the opposing team from gaining runs. The opposing team can be prevented from scoring any points by the fielding team delivering the ball is to hit the wicket so the bails fall, causing the batsman to be dismsised. The bowler can only deliver six balls at one wicket in a row, which wil; complete an "over". After an over, another bowler will complete another over of six balls whilst the batting side defends its wicket. For the remaining 9 'fielder' players, their role is to distribute around to catch and deliver the ball back to the pitch.
