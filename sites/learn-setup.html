Setting up the game

To run a game of cricket, there are three main areas of organisation needed: First, the equipment to play the game is required. Second, the field & pitch must be organised. Finally, the stratergy of gameplay should also be decided, within teams.

Equipment

Cricket requires a few pieces of specialised equipment to play in a safe and effective manner. To play a full game, you will need atleast 6 stumps, 4 bails, 2 cricket bats and 1 ball. Cricket bats are a large, wooden bat that is flat on one face, and rounded on the other (To give the bat strength). PLayers hit the ball with the flat side of the bat, to create the best distance possible for the hit. Runs will not be counted if hit from the curved side of the bat. The stumps and Bails are wooden components that used to create the wicket. The cricket ball, is a ball sewn with a straight line creating two equal hemispheres. Gear is required for the wicket keeper to protect the player from impact when catching the pitched ball. See Content for more detail on Equipment used in games.

Question: How is a cricket ball constructed differently from other sports balls?

Answer: The ball has a solid, sewn line splitting it into two halves. Did you know? Cricket balls are often red with white stiching for visibility, however during games that go into the night, a white ball is used.

The Field

The field is often a large, oval-shaped space with a rectangular strip in the center (the pitch). The field should be marked with a boundary line around the edge of the field. For casual games, The field can be a non oval-shape. The field has two postiion catergories: Offside & Legside The Offside - which is the side to the batsman's left and the opposing bowler's right. The Legside- (Onside) is the field on the batsman's right. The Distrubution of players for the fielding team is determined by these two sides, and the dominant hand of the batter to determine where best to postion players to intercept the ball when hit.

The Pitch

The Pitch

To set up the pitch, a zone of 20.1m long by 3m wide should be created in the middle of the field. The pitch should be divided into areas called 'Creases'. There are Four types of creases: -

  • The popping Crease (Otherwise known as the Batting Crease). This crease marks the boundary where the runner is safe from being run out. (taken out of play by the fielding team).
  • The return creases (two in the pitch) run along the side from the popping prese to the end of the pitch
  • The bowling crease runs parallel to the popping crease, inbetween the 2 return creases.
  • Each end of the pitch is marked with the last crease.

Question: How many Lines are on a cricket pitch?

Answer: 8! There are Four lines on each side of the pitch - Marking the Creases where the batters run, wicket is placed and where the ball is pitched from.

The Wickets

The Wickets are two structures constructed from 3 sticks, called stumps. The stumps are driven into the ground, with 2 bails (pieces across the wickets) set in divots between each pair. Wickets should be around 72.4cm high, and 22.9cm wide (between all three stumps). The center of the wickets are placed at the center of the each bowling crease.
